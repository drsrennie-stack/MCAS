Prototype Specifications

How the course should be organized. Pick one prototype per course.

Seven structural templates for the MCAS Engine. Each one locks the module rhythm, navigation pattern, assessment architecture, RSI scaffolding, and accessibility floor so faculty don't have to redesign these from scratch. The Engine reads your selection from the Master Course Workbook (tab 01) and applies the matching architecture to your content.

Which prototype is right for me?

Pick the closest match. You can switch later, but the prototype shapes everything downstream.

Clinical reasoning, professional programChoose Case-Based.
4 to 8-week intensiveChoose Accelerated.

Auditing or refactoring an existing courseChoose Course Redesign.

Prototypes

Prototype 01

Traditional Online

Fully online, asynchronous, content-driven. Individual learners progress through weekly modules at their own pace within a fixed term.

When to use

Identity

  • Pick this when the course is fully online, students cannot meet synchronously, and content delivery is the dominant mode.
  • Don't pick this if you need team-based application, in-person labs, or competency mastery gating.
  • Pedagogical bias: independent study with structured retrieval and one or two summative anchors.

Module rhythm

Weekly module structure

Overview → Read / Watch → Retrieval quiz → Application activity → Discussion → Wrap-up

One module per week. Fixed sequence. Each module opens Monday and closes Sunday. Students see what's open, what's locked, and what's overdue.

Navigation

Course shell layout

  • Home page: course banner, weekly checklist, "What's due this week," contact, RSI announcement zone.
  • Module page: linear navigation with a visible progress bar. Locked / unlocked / completed states.
  • Persistent sidebar: outcomes map, calendar, instructor presence indicator.
  • Pattern: top-down. Every page anchors to outcomes; every asset shows what outcomes it serves.

Assessment architecture

Weight distribution

Weekly 40%
Disc 15%
Mid 20%
Final 25%

Weekly quizzes 40% Discussions 15% Midterm 20% Final 25%

  • Frequent low-stakes retrieval. Weekly quizzes are short (10 to 15 items), auto-graded, with one retake.
  • Midterm and final use mixed item types: MCQ, short answer, scenario.
  • Discussion grading uses a clear rubric. No "any post counts" scoring.

Engagement signature

How students stay connected

  • Weekly threaded discussion tied to a discipline-relevant prompt.
  • Async office hours via a forum or scheduled video drop-ins.
  • Instructor-led announcements every Monday morning previewing the week.
  • Two-deep replies: students post once, reply substantively to two peers.

RSI pattern

Regular and substantive interaction

  • Weekly announcement (Monday): preview, framing, instructor voice.
  • 48-hour response window on student questions and email.
  • Individualized feedback on midterm and final, plus on at least one discussion per module.
  • Live touchpoint at least once per term (synchronous Zoom drop-in, optional but documented).

Compliance note: Traditional Online is the highest-risk prototype for RSI failure. Every interaction must be initiated by the instructor and be substantive (not a canned reply). Document evidence on workbook tab 11.

Accessibility floor

Highest-risk WCAG criteria

Risk areaWhat to verify
Video captions (1.2.2)Every video has accurate, edited captions. Auto-captions are not enough.
Video transcripts (1.2.1)Plain-text transcript downloadable for every video.
Color contrast (1.4.3)Slide decks, infographics, PDFs all hit 4.5:1 for normal text.
Document structure (1.3.1)PDFs are tagged. DOCX uses heading styles, not bold-as-heading.
Keyboard navigation (2.1.1)Every interactive element on every page is operable with Tab + Enter.

Compliance gotchas

What faculty miss

Gotcha: "Regular" without "substantive" is a finding. Auto-announcements and gradebook-triggered messages do not count as RSI. Plan one substantive instructor-initiated interaction per module.

Gotcha: Discussion rubrics that reward word count over reasoning produce shallow engagement and audit findings. Score for synthesis, not volume.

Prototype 02

Hybrid

Split delivery: asynchronous online prep plus in-person synthesis. Pre / In / Post cycle each week.

When to use

Identity

  • Pick this when students meet in person at least once per week and async work fills the gap between sessions.
  • Don't pick this if you need team cohesion (use TBL) or fully self-paced (use Competency-Based).
  • Pedagogical bias: flipped classroom. Content prep happens online; class time is for application and synthesis.

Module rhythm

Weekly Pre / In / Post cycle

Pre-class prep (online) → Readiness check → In-class application → Post-class consolidation

Each week is one cycle. Pre-class work opens 7 days before class. Readiness check closes 1 hour before. In-class time is application. Post-class consolidates with retrieval practice or reflection.

Navigation

Course shell layout

  • Home page: weekly cycle banner, "This week's class meeting" highlight, Pre / In / Post links.
  • Weekly module: three clearly labeled sections: Pre-class, In-class, Post-class.
  • Pattern: visual differentiation between online and in-person components. Color or icon cues for which mode each item lives in.

Assessment architecture

Weight distribution

Online 25%
In-class 25%
Mid 20%
Final 30%

Online quizzes 25% In-class activities 25% Midterm 20% Final 30%

  • Online readiness quizzes gate full participation in in-class application.
  • In-class activities scored for participation + product (small group worksheet, drawing, problem set).
  • Midterm and final test integration across pre and in-class material.

Engagement signature

How students stay connected

  • Readiness checks before each in-class meeting.
  • In-class small groups applying pre-class material.
  • Async discussion threading between class meetings.
  • Weekly recap connecting in-class observations back to outcomes.

RSI pattern

Regular and substantive interaction

  • In-person presence handles substantive interaction during class meetings (logged automatically).
  • Online feedback required on readiness checks and post-class reflections.
  • Weekly announcement bridges from last in-class to next pre-class.
  • Office hours offered both in-person and via Zoom.

Accessibility floor

Highest-risk WCAG criteria

Risk areaWhat to verify
Dual-format equity (1.3.1)Students who miss the in-person session can access equivalent material online.
Captions for recorded class (1.2.2)If class meetings are recorded, captions required within 5 business days.
In-class handouts (1.4.3, 1.3.1)Handouts pre-distributed in accessible HTML, not just printed in class.
Polling and clickers (4.1.2)Polling tools must have accessible alternatives for students using AT.

Default deliverable formats

Pre-class prepHTML (lecture page or reading)
Readiness checkHTML (LMS-native)
In-class slidesHTML or PPTX (with accessible export)
In-class handoutsHTML (preferred) or accessible DOCX
Post-class consolidationHTML retrieval tool or reflection prompt

MCAS Engine outputs

What the Engine generates from this prototype

  • Weekly Pre / In / Post module template
  • Readiness check scaffolds
  • In-class activity worksheet templates
  • Post-class consolidation prompts
  • In-person attendance log
  • Recording captions workflow checklist

Compliance gotchas

What faculty miss

Gotcha: Duplicating content across online and in-person modes wastes student time. Differentiate: pre-class introduces, in-class applies, post-class consolidates. Same content in two modes is a design failure.

Gotcha: Missing-class policy must offer accessible asynchronous equivalent. "You had to be there" is not compliant with disability accommodation.

Prototype 03

Team-Based Learning

Permanent teams. Readiness Assurance + 4S Application. Content prep is individual; class time is collective problem-solving.

When to use

Identity

  • Pick this when application matters more than recall, students meet in-person or sync hybrid, and team cohesion is a goal.
  • Don't pick this for fully async or for content too procedural for problem-solving.
  • Pedagogical bias: high-stakes accountability for prep, collective sense-making in class, frequent peer evaluation.

Module rhythm

TBL session structure

Pre-class prep → iRAT → tRAT → Appeals → Mini-lecture (as needed) → 4S Application → Gallery defense

4S design: Significant problem, Same problem for all teams, Specific choice (not open-ended), Simultaneous reveal. Skipping any of the four S's is the most common TBL failure.

Navigation

Course shell layout

  • Home page: team roster, current unit, readiness phase, next class.
  • Unit page: Prep, RAT, Application phases visible as a phased process.
  • Team page: private team workspace, peer evaluation portal, appeals log.

Assessment architecture

Weight distribution

iRAT 10%
tRAT 10%
Application 40%
Exams 30%
Peer 10%

iRAT 10% tRAT 10% 4S Application 40% Individual exams 30% Peer evaluation 10%

  • iRAT and tRAT use the same items. Individual first, then team.
  • Confidence-based scoring (IF-AT cards or equivalent) lets teams partially recover on incorrect first picks.
  • Peer evaluation is required and grade-bearing; without it, teams free-ride.

Engagement signature

Drawing-based synthesis

Application activities use visual reasoning. Teams draw, label, and defend on whiteboards or large paper. Gallery walks expose every team's reasoning simultaneously. "Given not Googled" is enforced by drawing in real time.

  • 4S application problems per unit, drawn or built in class.
  • Gallery defense: 90 seconds per team to explain their answer.
  • Peer probing: teams cross-examine before the instructor reveals.

RSI pattern

Regular and substantive interaction

  • Instructor circulates during application phase; substantive probing of team reasoning.
  • Appeals process after tRAT: teams submit written challenge with reasoning; instructor responds publicly.
  • Mini-lectures calibrated to gaps revealed by RAT performance.
  • Peer evaluation debrief midterm and end-of-term.

Accessibility floor

Highest-risk WCAG criteria

Risk areaWhat to verify
RAT delivery (1.3.1, 2.1.1)If RATs are on paper, screen reader users need a digital equivalent. If digital, screen reader-compatible item types only.
Gallery walks (1.4.5)Wall-displayed work needs an accessible digital copy for students with mobility or vision constraints.
Team workspaces (2.1.1)Shared docs and whiteboards must be navigable by keyboard and screen reader.
Peer evaluation (1.3.1)Evaluation forms accessible; comments visible to evaluated team member.

Default deliverable formats

What ships as what

AssetDefault format
Pre-class prepHTML reading or short video
iRAT / tRATHTML (digital, accessible) OR paper with IF-AT cards
4S problemsHTML problem cards (one per session)
Application worksheetsHTML (preferred) for accessibility, optional print
Peer evaluationHTML form (LMS or custom)

MCAS Engine outputs

What the Engine generates from this prototype

  • TBL session template (Prep + RAT + Application + Gallery)
  • RAT item generator (with answer key)
  • 4S application problem template (with 4S checklist)
  • Peer evaluation form (Michaelsen or modified)
  • Appeals log template
  • Team roster page

Compliance gotchas

What faculty miss

Gotcha: "TBL-ish" courses skip the 4S discipline and end up running case studies with extra steps. If the problem is open-ended, not specific-choice, or teams reveal at different times, it isn't TBL. The Engine will flag the 4S audit on workbook tab 12.

Gotcha: Peer evaluation without consequence (just informational) does not produce team accountability. The Michaelsen model requires it to be grade-bearing.

Prototype 04

Case-Based

Cases drive the content. Students reason through patient, client, or scenario unfolding over a unit. Clinical and professional programs.

When to use

Identity

  • Pick this when clinical or applied reasoning is the dominant skill, and concepts are best understood embedded in context.
  • Don't pick this when content is foundational and prerequisite to reasoning (use Traditional Online or Hybrid first).
  • Pedagogical bias: contextualized learning. Concept introduced through the case, not before it.

Module rhythm

Case progression

Case opens → Initial analysis → New data drops → Revised analysis → Resolution → Expert reveal

Cases unfold over a week or unit. New data introduces complexity. Students refine their reasoning rather than answer once and move on.

Navigation

Course shell layout

  • Home page: active case dashboard, "this week's case" framing.
  • Case page: patient card or scenario card at top, progressive disclosure of new data, prompts at each phase.
  • Pattern: case-driven sequencing, not topic-driven. Concepts surface when the case demands them.

Assessment architecture

Weight distribution

Case analyses 50%
Mid case 20%
Final case 30%

Weekly case analyses 50% Midterm case 20% Final case 30%

  • Case analyses graded with a consistent rubric: hypothesis, data interpretation, plan, justification.
  • Midterm and final cases are novel and individual.
  • Cases must be original or sufficiently modified to be non-Googleable.

Engagement signature

How students stay connected

  • Case discussion in person or async forum.
  • Role-play moments (student as clinician, family, advocate).
  • Expert reveal videos at case resolution.
  • Reflection on what reasoning shifted and why.

RSI pattern

Regular and substantive interaction

  • Probing questions from instructor during case discussion (not just confirming correctness).
  • Individualized feedback on each case write-up with reasoning audit.
  • Live debrief after each case resolution.
  • Office hours framed as "case consultation."

Accessibility floor

Highest-risk WCAG criteria

Risk areaWhat to verify
Case images and charts (1.1.1)Detailed alt text describing what the image conveys clinically, not "image of x-ray."
Patient or scenario cards (1.3.1)Structured semantically (headings, labeled data fields) not just visual layout.
Progressive disclosure (4.1.3)aria-live regions or focus management announce new case data when it drops.
Video case scenarios (1.2.2, 1.2.5)Captions and audio description where visual detail carries meaning.

Default deliverable formats

What ships as what

AssetDefault format
Case packetHTML (progressive disclosure) or accessible DOCX
Case analysisHTML form or DOCX submission
RubricsHTML (preferred for accessibility)
Expert revealHTML page with video + transcript
ReflectionHTML form or short discussion post

MCAS Engine outputs

What the Engine generates from this prototype

  • Case template (patient or scenario card + progressive data + prompts)
  • Reasoning rubric (hypothesis, data, plan, justification)
  • Case analysis submission form
  • Expert reveal page template
  • Reflection prompt scaffolds

Compliance gotchas

What faculty miss

Gotcha: Reused public cases (USMLE banks, published case books) appear in AI training data and Google results. Cases must be original or substantially modified, or integrity collapses.

Gotcha: Image-heavy cases without alt text exclude students using screen readers. Detailed alt is non-negotiable.

Prototype 05

Accelerated

Compressed term: 4 to 8 weeks. Daily or twice-weekly pacing. Same outcomes, half the calendar.

When to use

Identity

  • Pick this for summer intensives, 8-week sessions, or compressed certification programs.
  • Don't pick this if the same content is offered in a full term simultaneously and students can choose either. Accelerated has its own design demands.
  • Pedagogical bias: spaced retrieval to combat compressed forgetting, front-loaded scaffolding, ruthless prioritization.

Module rhythm

Daily or twice-weekly cycle

Daily overview → Focused input (60-90 min) → Retrieval practice → Application → Spaced review

Each day is its own micro-module. Retrieval practice from previous day baked in. Cumulative spaced review every 3 days.

Navigation

Course shell layout

  • Home page: today's date, today's module, urgent items, exam countdown.
  • Daily module: tight 5-section template, every day the same shape.
  • Pattern: visible pacing. "You are on day 12 of 32." Predictability reduces cognitive load in a compressed term.

Assessment architecture

Weight distribution

Daily 25%
App 25%
Mid 20%
Final 30%

Daily retrieval 25% Application 25% Midterm 20% Final 30%

  • Daily retrieval is short (5 to 10 items, 10 minutes max).
  • Applications are concise (1 problem per day).
  • Midterm and final are cumulative; rule out cramming-only success.

Engagement signature

How students stay connected

  • Daily check-in from instructor: voice memo, short video, or written.
  • Drawing-based retrieval (low time cost, high integrity value).
  • Cohort pacing with whole-class progress markers.
  • Wellness reminders: compressed terms drive burnout.

RSI pattern

Regular and substantive interaction

  • Daily presence required: every weekday has an instructor touch.
  • 24-hour response window on student questions (tighter than full-term).
  • Feedback within 48 hours on graded work; otherwise grades arrive after relevance has passed.
  • Live touchpoint at least once per week (sync class meeting or Zoom drop-in).

Accessibility floor

Highest-risk WCAG criteria

Risk areaWhat to verify
Front-loaded complianceNo time to remediate mid-term. Every module published accessible from day 1.
Reading load (3.1.5)Plain language critical; compressed timelines amplify cognitive load.
Captions on rapid content (1.2.2)If videos drop daily, caption workflow must keep pace.
Pacing transparency (3.2.3)Predictable layout day-to-day reduces navigational fatigue.

Default deliverable formats

What ships as what

AssetDefault format
Daily lectureHTML page with short video (10 to 15 min) + transcript
ReadingsHTML, chunked into 15-minute segments
Retrieval quizHTML (LMS-native), auto-graded with answer key
ApplicationHTML drawing or short problem
ExamsHTML or paper, proctored per institution policy

MCAS Engine outputs

What the Engine generates from this prototype

  • Daily module template (consistent shape across the term)
  • Spaced review scheduler (3-day, 7-day, 14-day intervals)
  • Daily retrieval generator
  • Cumulative midterm and final builder
  • Pacing calendar (visible day-by-day plan)
  • Wellness check-in cadence

Compliance gotchas

What faculty miss

Gotcha: Copying a full-term course into an 8-week shell breaks. Pace, sequencing, and assessment all need redesign. Accelerated is not "the same course, faster."

Gotcha: Faculty burnout. Daily presence in a 4 to 8-week term is sustainable only with batched feedback, async announcements scheduled in advance, and clear office-hour blocks.

Prototype 06

Competency-Based

Mastery learning. Students progress when they demonstrate the competency, not when the calendar says so.

When to use

Identity

  • Pick this for professional certification, skills-based programs, or self-paced graduate work.
  • Don't pick this when accreditation requires fixed-term grade reporting and you can't get mastery thresholds approved.
  • Pedagogical bias: demonstrated mastery over seat time. Retakes allowed and expected.

Module rhythm

Competency cluster progression

Learn → Practice → Self-check → Mastery checkpoint → Portfolio entry

Competencies organize the course, not weeks. Each competency has its own learn-practice-master loop. Students may be on different competencies simultaneously.

Navigation

Course shell layout

  • Home page: competency map (visual progress tracker), portfolio entry, coaching schedule.
  • Competency page: learning resources, practice tasks, mastery checkpoint, prior attempts.
  • Pattern: non-linear. Students can work on any unlocked competency.

Assessment architecture

Weight distribution

Mastery checkpoints 60%
Portfolio 25%
Coaching 15%

Mastery checkpoints 60% Portfolio 25% Coaching engagement 15%

  • Mastery checkpoints scored pass/redo, not letter-grade per attempt.
  • Portfolio collects evidence across competencies; submitted at term end.
  • Coaching attendance is required and grade-bearing (counts as RSI evidence).

Engagement signature

How students stay connected

  • 1:1 coaching sessions scheduled by competency, not week.
  • Mastery groups: peer accountability for students on the same competency.
  • Public progress map showing cohort momentum.
  • Reflection on attempts (what shifted between attempt 1 and 2?).

RSI pattern

Regular and substantive interaction

  • Coaching session per competency at minimum.
  • Substantive feedback on every mastery attempt, pass or redo.
  • Weekly cohort touchpoint (announcement or sync).
  • Portfolio reviews midpoint and term end.

Accessibility floor

Highest-risk WCAG criteria

Risk areaWhat to verify
Competency map (1.3.1, 4.1.2)Visual map must have an accessible text-based alternative listing competencies and status.
Progress dashboard (4.1.3)State changes (competency unlocked, mastery achieved) announced via aria-live.
Portfolio submission (1.3.1)Portfolio tool must support keyboard, screen reader, and multiple submission formats.
Retake equity (3.3.4)Clear error identification on redo attempts so students learn from failures.

Default deliverable formats

What ships as what

AssetDefault format
Learning resources per competencyHTML page (short, focused)
Practice tasksHTML, unscored, with feedback
Mastery checkpointsHTML (preferred) or in-person performance
PortfolioHTML hub + DOCX or PDF artifacts
Coaching notesHTML form (kept private to faculty and student)

MCAS Engine outputs

What the Engine generates from this prototype

  • Competency map page
  • Per-competency learn-practice-master template
  • Mastery checkpoint scaffold (with rubric and retake policy)
  • Portfolio scaffold
  • Coaching session note template
  • Cohort progress dashboard

Compliance gotchas

What faculty miss

Gotcha: Retake policy. Unlimited attempts often produces gaming; zero retakes defeats the model. Codify the policy (e.g., 2 attempts with mandatory coaching before the third).

Gotcha: Mastery threshold definition. "Pass" needs a defensible criterion (rubric score, accuracy threshold, expert sign-off). Without it, the model collapses into pass-anyone or fail-anyone.

Prototype 07

Course Redesign / Program Review

Not building new. Auditing, aligning, and remediating an existing course or program against quality, accessibility, and RSI standards.

When to use

Identity

  • Pick this when the course already exists and the work is audit, alignment, and remediation rather than greenfield design.
  • Don't pick this if you're starting from scratch; pick the prototype that matches the new course's modality.
  • Pedagogical bias: evidence-based audit. Decisions backed by alignment matrices, compliance scans, and student outcome data.

Redesign rhythm

Audit and remediation phases

Discover → Audit → Plan → Implement → Verify → Certify

Not weekly modules. Phase-based workflow. Each phase has deliverables and gates.

Navigation

Workspace layout

  • Home page: course under review, current phase, overall readiness score.
  • Phase page: audit checklist, evidence captured, remediation backlog.
  • Pattern: diagnostic and project-management oriented, not pedagogical delivery.

Audit areas (not student assessment)

Engagement signature

How stakeholders stay connected

  • Stakeholder interviews (faculty, students, accreditors).
  • Peer review by a fellow faculty member or instructional designer.
  • Public remediation backlog so progress is visible.
  • Certification milestone announced when readiness threshold met.

RSI in the audit

Documenting and improving RSI

  • Inventory current RSI (what evidence exists today).
  • Identify gaps (modules, weeks, or interactions with no documented evidence).
  • Plan remediation (additions or replacements with stronger evidence).
  • Verify post-implementation.

MCAS Engine outputs

Compliance gotchas

What audits miss

Gotcha: Scope creep. A redesign that tries to fix everything fixes nothing. Pick 3 to 5 prioritized findings per phase and ship.

Gotcha: Sacred cows. The signature assignment that "everyone loves" may not be aligned to outcomes. Be willing to retire it.

Gotcha: Accreditor deadlines. Map the redesign timeline to accreditor cycles so the work counts toward review, not against it.
